# 90 Million Users Deserve a Better App

![Firebase Performance dashboard showing iOS app start time dropping from 4 seconds to 976 milliseconds on March 19](/images/blog/firebase-performance.png)

It took me a week to run the app on my first day. ELSA has 90 million users.

I'm a Flutter developer. Before ELSA I was the sole mobile engineer at a social-media marketplace at Cohart, and before that I freelanced for years. The foundation work, mostly. Setting up the first month of an app's life: the architecture, the patterns, the things that decide whether a codebase stays clean or rots. I got opinionated about it.

I joined ELSA two years ago, expecting to learn something. Maybe big companies do things better. Maybe a team that ships to that many people knows something I don't. Maybe I could help.

I was wrong about the first two.

---

## What I walked into

The app was in the middle of a migration. About 30% done. Two years in.

It wasn't really one app. It was two apps shipped as one binary. The original was ten years old and fully native, with planet-themed lessons; the team called it Classic. The newer one was AI-driven with a learning path, hybrid Flutter and native through a wrapper; we called it Nova. After login, the server decided which app each user got. Classic was shrinking. Nova was growing.

About a hundred screens total across the two. Maybe ten of them were Flutter. The rest were Swift and Java, owned by different teams, sometimes by different teams on different platforms for the same feature.

To run any of it, you cloned three separate repos.

To develop a new Flutter screen, you ran a fake Flutter app that contained just that one screen, because hot reload didn't work in the real build. To see what users would actually see, you opened Xcode or Android Studio, waited two and a half hours for `pub get` and codegen, and then ran the native build, where hot reload still didn't work.

Two engineers in the entire company could build the Android app successfully. There was a wiki page about the linking scripts. The wiki page had been updated 14 times.

Native owned navigation. Flutter and native communicated through custom `MethodChannel` bridges. Auth, sessions, remote config, paywall, onboarding. Five black boxes, each one designed by someone who'd moved on, each one impossible to debug from either side. `MissingPluginException` showed up in Sentry every day. Flutter error logs didn't appear in the native build at all.

The Flutter side wasn't simple either. 88 Melos packages. State management was a hand-rolled BLoC pattern built on RxDart, because at some point an engineer had written a 21-minute analysis comparing eight frameworks and concluded that `flutter_bloc` wasn't flexible enough. Navigation lived in a folder called `core_navigation`. There was also a folder called `core_navigation_v2`. We were building V3. The principal proposed V4.

If you could run the app on your first day, it was a miracle.

---

## What I learned early

There is a story big companies tell about themselves: *we go native because we have the resources to maintain separate codebases.* It is a lie. What you actually get is two teams building two products that share a name. Features fall behind on one platform. Decisions made on Android get re-litigated on iOS by people who don't remember the first conversation. Engineers burn out trying to keep parity. Vendors get brought in to plug gaps and then get blamed for the gaps they didn't make.

ELSA's lifetime rating on the App Store is 4.7 stars. The native app, built over ten years, had earned that. During the migration, the new reviews coming in averaged under 2 stars and stayed there for months. They came in six languages and said the same things. One of them, in Japanese: アップデートでとても使いにくくなってしまった. The update made it very difficult to use. Features that had worked in the old version disappeared after the update. A vocabulary set somebody had built over months wasn't there anymore. A subscription paid for on the App Store didn't unlock anything inside the app. The new UI was harder to use than the old UI, and every update seemed to make it harder again. Every Flutter screen we shipped was replacing a native screen that had at least been working.

I didn't believe any of this on day one. By month two I did.

---

## The wall

You know the engineer who always wants to rewrite everything? I'm that engineer. I know it's a red flag. But sometimes the house is not worth renovating. On my first day at ELSA, I knew. I also knew nobody would let me.

Changing code from another team was a red flag. You'd be labeled a troublemaker. Your manager would have to explain to the other team that the new guy was just trying to help.

Decisions that had been made were sacred. People would hand me documentation that began with the line *"After careful consideration."* I tried writing my own proposals. Nobody in the org wanted a big change anyway; the cost of agreeing to one was higher than the cost of staying broken. The first few went unread. Long documents had stopped getting read by then. Everyone had started assuming, fairly, that anything over a page had been written by a chatbot. I skimmed other people's. They skimmed mine.

So I talked instead. In every meeting. The principal during 1:1s, every dev I could pin down at a standup. I started joking that if any of them ever went on a long vacation, the code would already be different by the time they got back. Stealth refactor. They laughed. I think some of them knew I was serious.

A single design call traveled engineer → manager → director → another director in a different branch → manager → engineer. Decisions stopped being decisions and started being agreements. Those are not the same thing.

The vendors were in the mix too. They weren't bad. They were contractors hired to ship features through the bridge layer nobody wanted to touch. Occasionally they raised the same concerns I did. Nobody listened. They were honest, and they were the least empowered people in the room.

---

## Earning trust the hard way

Words didn't work. Documents didn't work. What worked was shipping.

The first thing I was allowed to architect on my own was the game module. I designed it the way I'd design something on day one of any project. Simple, practical, scalable without being clever about it. People used it. People said *this actually makes sense now*.

The second thing was Navigation V3. Yes, we needed a third version. V3 is a thin wrapper around `go_router`. That's the whole product. The principal had a different idea for V4: event-based, pluggable, flexible enough that the same navigation system could also run on the web. I said no. We had just spent two years paying for the last "flexible" architecture. Flexibility is a bill you pay forever. In onboarding time. In bug triage. In the half hour every new engineer spends figuring out which layer of indirection actually triggers a page change.

---

## The light at the end of the tunnel

Then layoffs hit. Half the team. More people left on their own afterward.

Paradoxically, this is when things started moving. My team became critical. My manager, Duc, gave me real trust and real autonomy. The five-person decision chain shortened to one person, and that person was usually him, and his answer was usually *try it.*

The other thing that changed was the tooling. AI agents were getting good. The peak was Opus 4.5. Not for the architecture. That had to come from someone who'd lived in the codebase. But the architecture was never what slowed me down. What slowed me down was the **typing**.

Rewriting 6,538 `package:` imports to relative imports across 2,084 files used to be a project plan. It became a script with teeth. It could read errors, retry, and tell me when it was stuck. I read every PR. I caught it being wrong three times. Then I let it grind.

One failure stuck with me. The agent wrote a Python script to do a find-and-replace. The script kept breaking. I killed it, opened the find and replace panel in VS Code, add some regex, and finished the job in a minute. AI just wants to write code.

Finding every place a MethodChannel was being called from used to be two days with grep and a spreadsheet. It became a list I could read before lunch. The decisions were still mine.

The typing used to be the bottleneck. Now I am. Because I read every diff, and I can only read code so fast. The tool can write faster than I can review. That's the actual limit, and I think it's the right kind of limit to have.

The migration that "couldn't be done" started moving fast. Really fast.

---

## The day it happened

It came down to one merge to main. The squash commit's hash is `2d7eb15c7`. The message ends with the part that mattered: *"remove classic."* It changed 4,929 files and removed 280,648 lines of code.

That commit only worked because other engineers had spent months porting screens out of native and into Flutter, one at a time. By the time I deleted the shells, there was a Flutter version of every screen waiting on the other side. That part was theirs.

That was the day the native shells died. Not deprecated. Deleted from the build on March 4, 2026.

The Sentry graph stopped lighting up with `MissingPluginException`. The bridge was gone because there was nothing left to bridge to.

App start time on iOS dropped from 3.6 seconds to 976 milliseconds. 73% faster. We had been paying a 1.5-second tax on every cold start, every day, for years, while the native shell booted and the method channel registered itself. We stopped paying it.

The download got 30% smaller on both platforms.

`pub get` and codegen used to take two and a half hours. Now it takes three minutes. CI dropped from half an hour to under ten. Full deploy from over an hour to about thirty. `make bootstrap` finishes in under thirty minutes on day one. The miracle is now normal.

---

## The outcome

88 packages, gone. One `pubspec.yaml`. One source of truth. No more finger-pointing about which package owns what.

6,538 imports rewritten across 2,084 files, and another 1,057 across the test tree. Every single one of them had been a tiny piece of debt someone left behind thinking *someone else will fix it later.* I was that someone else.

The classic native app is dead. The two native shell repos are sitting in archive. One Flutter codebase for Android, iOS, and web.

The vendors that nobody listened to were right about most things. I wish they were still around to see it.

![Contribution graph for chaucao-elsa: 256 commits, 823,077 lines added, 1,000,907 deleted](/images/blog/contribution.png)

Two years at ELSA. 256 commits. I added 823,077 lines of code. I deleted just over a million. The app got smaller. That's the number I'm most proud of. The boring kind of pride.

---

## What I take from this

Simplicity wins. Every single time. The "flexible and scalable" architecture that two careful years couldn't half-finish was replaced by something straightforward that just works.

Politics slow things down more than bad code ever will. The moment Duc gave me real autonomy, things moved 10x faster. Not because I'm 10x smarter. Decisions just stopped traveling through five people who each added their own careful consideration.

---

## What I leave behind

I told someone, early on, that I might leave before making a dent. The dent is made. The thing I came here to do is done.

Duc, my engineering manager, gave me real autonomy after the team shrank. He stopped routing decisions through five people. The thing we shipped is also his.

90 million users deserved a better app. Now they have one.
