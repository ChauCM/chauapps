# I Deleted a Million Lines of Code from an App 90 Million People Use

![Firebase Performance dashboard showing iOS app start time dropping from 4 seconds to 976 milliseconds on March 19](/images/blog/firebase-performance.png)

It took me a week to run the app on my first day.

I joined ELSA two years ago expecting to learn something. They ship to 90 million people. I figured a team that big knew things I didn't. Two years later the squash commit that ended my project deleted 280,648 lines and got the cold start down from 3.6 seconds to under one. The lesson I actually learned wasn't technical. It was that the thing standing between 90 million people and a working app was never the code. It was who was allowed to change it.

---

## Two apps in a trench coat

ELSA is an English-pronunciation app. You talk to it, it listens, and it tells you how close you sounded to a native speaker. That part has worked for years. The app wrapped around it is the part this story is about.

![A scripted AI conversation in ELSA, scoring a spoken answer — "I wake up at seven, take a shower, and have breakfast" — at 70%](/images/elsa-mobile.png)

The app wasn't one app. It was two, shipped as one binary.

The original was ten years old, fully native, planet-themed lessons; the team called it Classic. The newer one was AI-driven, half new code and half old, stitched together through a wrapper; we called it Nova. After login, a server decided which app you got. The migration from one to the other was about 30% done. It had been running for two years.

The day-one experience tells you everything. To run it, you cloned three repos. To build one new screen, you ran a fake app containing only that screen, because hot reload didn't work in the real build. To see what a user would see, you opened the native IDE, waited two and a half hours for dependencies to resolve, and got a build where hot reload still didn't work. Two engineers in the whole company could build the Android version. The wiki page explaining how had been updated 14 times.

The two halves talked through custom bridges nobody owned. Auth, sessions, paywall, onboarding: black boxes, each designed by someone who had since left, each impossible to debug from either side. The crash dashboard logged the same bridge error every single day.

If you could run the app on your first day, it was a miracle.

---

## The lie

There's a story big companies tell about themselves: *we build native on each platform because we have the resources to maintain separate codebases.* It's a lie. What you get is two teams building two products that happen to share a name. Features fall behind on one side. A decision made on Android gets re-argued on iOS by people who weren't in the first conversation. Engineers burn out keeping parity. Contractors get hired to plug the gaps, then blamed for the gaps they didn't make.

You can read the lie in the reviews. ELSA's lifetime App Store rating is 4.7 stars, ten years of native work earning it. During the migration, the new reviews averaged under 2 and stayed there for months, in six languages, all saying the same thing. One, in Japanese:

> アップデートでとても使いにくくなってしまった — *The update made it very difficult to use.*

A vocabulary set someone had built over months, gone after an update. A subscription bought on the App Store that unlocked nothing. A new UI harder to use than the old one, getting harder with each release. Every screen we shipped was replacing a native screen that, whatever else was wrong with it, worked.

I didn't believe any of this on day one. By month two I did.

---

## You can't fix what you're not allowed to touch

I'm the engineer who always wants to rewrite everything. I know that's a red flag. But sometimes the house isn't worth renovating, and on my first day I knew this was one of those times. I also knew nobody would let me.

Touching another team's code marked you as a troublemaker. Decisions already made were sacred; people handed me docs that opened with *"After careful consideration."* I wrote proposals. Nobody read them, partly because nobody wanted a big change in the first place, and partly because long documents had stopped being read at all. Everyone assumed, fairly, that anything over a page came from a chatbot. I skimmed theirs. They skimmed mine.

So I stopped writing and started talking. Every meeting, every standup, every 1:1. I joked that if anyone took a long vacation, the code would be different by the time they got back. They laughed. Some of them knew I meant it.

A single design call traveled engineer → manager → director → another director in a different branch → manager → engineer. Somewhere in there, decisions stopped being decisions and turned into agreements. Those aren't the same thing.

The contractors lived in the worst of it. They weren't bad engineers; they were hired to ship features through the bridge layer nobody else would touch. Now and then they raised the exact concerns I had. Nobody listened. They were honest about it, and the least able to change anything.

---

## What actually moved it

Two things broke the stalemate, and neither was a better argument.

The first was layoffs. Half the team, then more who left on their own. It should have made things worse. Instead my team became load-bearing, and my manager, Duc, started handing me real autonomy. The five-person approval chain collapsed to one person, usually him, and his answer was usually *try it.* The migration that "couldn't be done" started moving the week the politics got out of the way.

The second was that the tooling got good. Not for the architecture — that still had to come from someone who'd lived in the codebase. The architecture was never the slow part. The typing was. Rewriting 6,538 imports across 2,084 files used to be something you'd schedule a sprint for. Claude Code is my daily driver now; I described the rule once and came back to a pull request I could actually read. Some of the rewrites were wrong. The rest just worked, and the decisions stayed mine.

I read every diff, which means I'm the bottleneck now. The tool writes faster than I can review. That's the right limit to have.

---

## Remove classic

It came down to one merge to main. The squash hash is `2d7eb15c7`. The commit message ends on the part that mattered: *"remove classic."* It changed 4,929 files and deleted 280,648 lines.

That merge only worked because other engineers had spent months porting screens out of native, one at a time, so that by the time I deleted the shells there was a working replacement waiting behind every one of them. That part was theirs.

The native shells died on March 4, 2026. Not deprecated. Deleted.

The bridge error vanished from the crash dashboard, because there was nothing left to bridge to. iOS cold start went from 3.6 seconds to 976 milliseconds, a 73% cut; we'd been paying a 1.5-second tax on every launch, every day, for years, while the native shell booted and the bridge registered itself. The download shrank 30% on both platforms. A clean build dropped from two and a half hours to three minutes. CI from thirty minutes to under ten. The 88 packages became one. Three repos became one codebase, for Android, iOS, and web.

![Contribution graph for chaucao-elsa: 256 commits, 823,077 lines added, 1,000,907 deleted](/images/blog/contribution.png)

Two years, 256 commits. I added 823,077 lines and deleted just over a million. The app got smaller. That's the number I'm most proud of, and it's the boring kind of pride.

---

I told someone early on I might leave before I made a dent. The dent is made, and Duc is the reason it was allowed to be; the thing we shipped is as much his as mine. I'm out in May, looking for the next broken thing.
